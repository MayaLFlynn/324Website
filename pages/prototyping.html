Prototyping

Identify relevant artifacts from the human-centered design process that address the following points:
What design questions did your intend to answer with your prototype(s).
How did you design your prototype(s) and subsequent user studies to address these questions?
What answers to your design questions did your efforts unveil?

Our paper prototype was a simple version of our game. It included a cut scene to demonstrate how the story would happen, and a simple layout of our game. The prototype was designed by all four of us in collaboration. I provided the materials, and suggested the mechanic of cutting a slit in the paper in order to adjust the sliders on the page. The layout and functionality was discussed on a whiteboard before putting the ideas down on the page.

We had a few questions that we wanted to answer with our prototype. We wanted to know if the initial mechanics made sense, if the story felt like it matched the visuals, and if the story felt compelling. Since I was working on the mechanics of the game, I was most interested in the responses that involved mechanics. To do this, we designed the interactable pieces of the prototype to pop off the page, so it would be more visually obvious that they were interactable. We also had all of the mechanics that would be available to the user in the first stages of the game available, but didn't add the mechanics and buttons that the user wouldn't be able to interact with until end game, because we wanted to know how users interacted with what was available, and didn't want to crowd the space with options.

In the end, we found that users felt like mechanics made sense. One user wanted sound effects to know if what they were doing had any effect. Another pointed out that if this was the tutorial, the it may feel odd to switch to another world. We agreed with that suggestion, and decided against a tutorial. One user also wanted to click on everything at once to discover what was available. This was interesting and guided how we thought about the initial buttons.
